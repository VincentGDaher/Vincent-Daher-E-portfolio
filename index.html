Didactic analysis

Conceptual tools for interpreting mathematics teaching

Each concept connects an academic lens to observed classroom practice and future decisions.

Teacher - student - knowledge

Triangle didactique

The triangle didactique studies relationships between teacher, student, and mathematical knowledge.

Observation notes can identify whether explanation, student activity, or formalization dominated a moment.

Future lessons should balance guidance with student engagement in the knowledge itself.
Expectations

Contrat didactique

The contrat didactique refers to implicit expectations between teacher and students.

Students may wait for teacher confirmation if the classroom contract encourages dependence.

A stronger contract can invite students to justify, test, and correct reasoning.
Knowledge transformation

Transposition didactique

Transposition didactique studies how scholarly knowledge becomes teachable school knowledge.

Definitions, exercises, and examples simplify or reorganize mathematical knowledge.

Lesson design should protect mathematical meaning while making content accessible.
Learning indicators

Errors and obstacles

Errors may reveal epistemological, didactic, or cognitive obstacles.

Repeated mistakes in a class can become evidence for targeted analysis.

The teacher can transform error correction into conceptual learning.
Problem situations

Situations-problemes

A situation-probleme places students in a meaningful challenge that creates a need for new knowledge.

Observation can examine whether a problem creates intellectual activity.

Tasks should give students a reason to construct or adapt a strategy.
Formalization

Institutionalization

Institutionalization names, stabilizes, and recognizes knowledge as mathematical.

Teachers move from examples or student strategies to formal statements and accepted methods.

Clear institutionalization helps students know what must be retained and reused.
Learning conditions

Classroom management

Management supports the conditions that allow mathematical work to happen.

Routines, pacing, transitions, and attention signals affect engagement.

Management should serve learning goals and didactic choices.
Participation

Student engagement

Engagement includes attention, effort, participation, and intellectual investment.

Observation can track which tasks generate meaningful participation.

Engagement improves when tasks are accessible, purposeful, and demanding.
Feedback

Formative assessment

Formative assessment gathers information during learning to support progress.

Short checks, questioning, and feedback reveal understanding before final assessment.

Assessment should guide both student improvement and teacher planning.
